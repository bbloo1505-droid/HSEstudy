# Study primer: WES, WEL and monitoring

[[page:exp-1]]

## Occupational hygiene cycle

**Anticipate → recognise → evaluate → control → verify.** Air monitoring sits mainly in evaluate and verify. Monitoring should be designed and interpreted by a competent person (often an occupational hygienist) when concentrations are uncertain or when monitoring is needed to determine whether there is a health risk.

## WES and WEL transition (critical for August 2026 start)

[[page:exp-2]]

- **Workplace Exposure Standards (WES)** — the list that remains legally operative for your August 2026 start date in this curriculum.
- **Workplace Exposure Limits (WEL)** — the replacement list that begins on **1 December 2026**.
- Learning both is useful; always label which list is currently legally operative when you talk or write.

Do not invent numeric limits in reports. Look up the current official list.

## TWA, STEL and peak

[[page:exp-3]]

- **TWA** — time-weighted average over a reference period (commonly 8 hours in many systems).
- **STEL** — short-term exposure limit over a short averaging period (often 15 minutes where defined).
- **Peak limitation** — a ceiling-style limit that should not be exceeded.

Personal monitoring samples the breathing zone of a worker. Static/area monitoring characterises a location. They answer different questions.

## Silica note

[[page:exp-4]]

Queensland’s silica code commenced on **1 May 2023** and provides control guidance for construction and manufacturing activities involving respirable crystalline silica. Controls come first; monitoring and health monitoring support the risk-management system — they do not replace elimination/engineering controls.

## Health monitoring versus air monitoring

[[page:exp-5]]

Air monitoring measures the work environment or exposure. Health monitoring examines effects on a worker’s health. Health monitoring does not replace controls and must be carried out or supervised by an appropriately experienced registered medical practitioner where required.
