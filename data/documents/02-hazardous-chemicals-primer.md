# Study primer: Hazardous chemicals

[[page:chem-1]]

## Hazard versus risk

A **hazard** is the inherent potential for harm (for example, a flammable liquid or a sensitiser). **Risk** is the chance that harm will occur when people, tasks and controls interact. Classification tells you about hazard; your site assessment tells you about risk.

## GHS, labels and SDSs

[[page:chem-2]]

- **GHS** provides a harmonised system of classification and communication using hazard classes, categories, pictograms, signal words and hazard/precautionary statements.
- **Labels** must communicate identity and hazards for containers in the workplace according to applicable requirements.
- **Safety data sheets (SDSs)** provide structured information (including hazards, first aid, firefighting, handling, exposure controls and regulatory information). Check that the SDS is current for the product in use.

## Registers, manifests and placards

[[page:chem-3]]

A **hazardous chemical register** is a list of hazardous chemicals used, handled or stored at the workplace, typically linked to current SDSs. Manifests and placards apply when quantities and types meet threshold criteria for emergency services information. Exact thresholds must be read from the current Regulation/code — do not rely on memory for reportable quantities.

## Exposure routes and health monitoring

[[page:chem-4]]

Common routes: inhalation, skin absorption/contact, ingestion, and injection/puncture. Effects may be **acute** (short-term) or **chronic** (longer-term or delayed). **Health monitoring** examines effects on a worker’s health and does not replace controls. It must be carried out or supervised by an appropriately experienced registered medical practitioner where required.

## Consultant relevance

When reviewing a chemical register, look for missing SDSs, outdated sheets, unnamed products, storage/compatibility issues, and unclear ownership of the register. Escalate gaps; do not invent compliance conclusions.
